# Product — Augment Cosmos

## What It Is

Augment Cosmos is the unified cloud agents platform with shared context and memory that compounds across the team and the software development lifecycle.

Company mission: We make software teams AI native.

Category-level theme we own (narrative, not product descriptor): Agentic SDLC — the outcome an engineering org reaches when agents work across the software development lifecycle as one coordinated system.

## Core Features

### Unified across the software development lifecycle
One platform where agents work across every tool, surface, and stage of software development. Agents trigger off events teams already work from (PRs, production alerts, Jira/Linear tickets, schedules, webhooks). Agents work where the team already does — CLI, web, Slack, mobile. New workflows can be set up in natural language, with no custom integration work per use case.

### Self-improving agents (compounding capability)
Built on a shared context engine and shared virtual filesystem with tenant-wide and private memory. Patterns, conventions, and corrections accumulate across sessions instead of resetting. Experts built by one engineer can be promoted into shared capabilities the whole org uses. Ships with a knowledge base of agent patterns refined across customer engagements.

### Enterprise scale
Augment's Context Engine grounds agents in large enterprise codebases, not toy repos. Agents and sessions run in parallel at high concurrent volume with consistent behavior under load. Long-running sessions persist across days and weeks without losing context. Designed for hundreds of concurrent agents per org.

### Enterprise control
Governed by default. Runs in your own environment or in our cloud sandboxes — your choice. Multi-model with BYOK across Anthropic, OpenAI, Bedrock, Vertex, and open-source. Teams configure where humans get pulled in for review, and the platform enforces those rules. Every agent action is observable and auditable.

## Off-Limits Claims

- Do NOT name Stripe, Ramp, or Uber as customers. They are referenced as examples of sophisticated orgs building this kind of system internally — they have no commercial relationship with Cosmos. No "trusted by," "used by," "including," or list-style constructions placing them alongside customer names.
- Do NOT use "Agentic software development platform" or "Agentic SDLC platform" as the product/category descriptor. The product is the Unified Cloud Agents Platform.
- Do NOT use "SDLC" as a bare acronym in broad customer-facing copy (ads, homepage, top-of-funnel). Spell out "software development lifecycle" or name the specific stage (review, testing, deployment, etc.).
- Do NOT use "AI transformation" or "AI-native transformation" (consultant/change-program language). "AI native" as a state/mission is fine; "AI transformation" as a program is not.
- Do NOT use "AI software engineer," "AI engineer," "droid," or any framing that names agents as replacements for humans. Replacement language reads as threatening to engineering leaders and lands badly.
- Do NOT describe Cosmos as a "coding agent." Coding is one of many things agents on Cosmos do.
- Do NOT use replacement language generally. Cosmos is about coordination, not substitution.
- Do NOT claim specific customer counts, named outcomes, or case studies — Cosmos is in design partner stage.
- Do NOT claim speed benchmarks unless provided.
- Do NOT use "AI-powered" — say what it actually does.

## Primary Buyer

CTO / VP Engineering at enterprise companies (ideally cloud-native). They've rolled out individual coding agents and are now feeling the breakdown — engineers buried in agent-generated PRs, quality dropping, no compounding. They want a system, not another point tool. CEO is often the economic buyer.

Secondary: Staff engineer / platform lead — the senior IC the CTO brings in to evaluate primitives and execute on initial workflows. Often already building a homegrown version.

## Pain Points (for hook/headline framing)

1. **Agent chaos** — overwhelmed by agent-generated PRs, quality dropping, more incidents
2. **Cost of point solutions** — stacking CodeRabbit + Cursor + others, paying for seats and tokens with no compounding return
3. **Fragmentation** — too many point tools, none of them talking to each other, no shared memory
